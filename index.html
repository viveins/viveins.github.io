    Vivek Padmanabha is an Assistant Professor of the Department of Civil Engineering (Geotechnical Division) at the Indian Institute of Technology Guwahati. He holds a Ph.D. (2018) degree in Civil Engineering from Indian Institute of Science (IISc), Bangalore, India. He obtained his Master degree, M.Tech. (2011) in Geotechnical Engineering from Indian Institute of Technology Kanpur (IITK), India, and the Bachelor degree, B.E. (2008) in Civil Engineering from BMS College of Engineering, Bengaluru, India.

    Before joining the Indian Institute of Technology Guwahati, he was a Postdoctoral Researcher from April 2019 to Dec 2021 in Department of General Geology and Structural Geology at Albert Ludwig University of Freiburg, Freiburg, Germany. During the above mentioned period, he also served as an academic scientist at Fraunhofer Institute for High-Speed Dynamics, Freiburg, Germany or popularly known as Ernst Mach Institute (EMI). From Mar 2018 - Mar 2019, he was as a Postdoctoral Research Associate at Indian Institute of Science, on an industry collaborative project with Sarathy Geotech And Engineering Services Pvt. Ltd, Bengaluru, India. Early in his career, he worked as a Research Assistant for short-term at BMS College of Engineering, India (2008) and University of Illinois, Chicago,, USA (2011).

    Vivek's research mentors at different stages of his research career include Prof. TG Sitharam, Prof. Frank Schäfer, Prof. Thomas Kenkmann, Prof. Priyanka Ghosh, and Prof. S Raghunath.

    Research Interests : Understanding the High strain-rate behavior of materials, particularly geomaterials like soil, rock, and concrete; Shock phenomena (attenuation) through granular and porous materials, which includes sand particles, porous rocks, synthetic geotextile materials and others ; Fracture and Fragmentation of rocks subjected to different loading rate from quasi-static events to very high velocity impacts. Adaptive mitigation strategies against extreme events and disasters like blast during war & terrorist attacks, chemical & nuclear explosion, landslides & debris flow, asteroid impacts etc. »

    Research Works

      2019-2021 High Strain-rate Effects and Fragmentation in Rocks

    • Brittle deformation at high strain rates (during fault rupture, landslide events, and meteorite impacts) results in intense fragmentation and rock pulverisation.

    • The strength behaviour during quasi-static and dynamic compression and tension tests are determined in the laboratory using Split Hopkinson Pressure and Tension Bar (SHPB & SHTB).

    • The fragment size shows a strong strain rate dependency for dynamic failure under uniaxial compression and indirect tension.

    • Published articles: [ pdf ]; [ pdf ]; [ pdf ].


      • 2016-18; 2020-21 Dynamic Strain Measurement in Soil and Rocks using Digital Image Correlation

    • Stress wave propagation in soils and corresponding strain measurements due to a simulated air-blast loading are estimated in the laboratory.

    • The digital image correlation (DIC) technique is used to evaluate longitudinal and lateral in-plane displacements and strains.

    • Visualisation of the sand deformation was possible by tracking the movement of the sand particle using a high-speed camera.

    • The dynamic strain obtained using DIC is correlated with correlates with the applied loading rate on rocks/ soil deposits.

    • Published articles: [ pdf ].

      • 2015-2016 Landmine Simulator

    • A landmine simulator was indigenously built to simulate buried blast loading in a laboratory.

    • Different stages of landmine explosion are simulated: Blast interaction phase, Bubble expansion phase and Soil ejecta phase.

    • Sand ejecta kinematics and impulse transfer to the target structure are analysed using an image processing technique with the aid of high-speed photography.

    • The shock wave emanating from the buried blast is visualised using shadowgraphy technique.

    • Published articles: [ pdf ].


      • 2015-2016 Blast Protective Structures

    • Geotextile encapsulated sand barrier systems are 'engineered versions of sandbags', used for blast mitigation.

    • A scaled laboratory tests are performed to evaluate the performance of the sand barrier systems.

    • The presence of geotextile significantly reduced the stress transfer and gas filtration process through the infill granular materials, thereby increasing the attenuation response.

    • Published articles: [ pdf ].


      • 2017-19; 2022- Numerical Simulation of Shock-Materails Interactions

    • Shock-geomaterials interaction finds application in blast mitigation and other geophysical problems like rock avalanches and debris flows.

    • Lagrangian and Eulerian solver available in ABAQUS is used to simulate the Shock-material interactions

    • Numerical results are validated against the shock tube experimental results.
      Shock tube simulation in Eulerian formulation- [video].

    • Understanding the complex interaction of granular materials or rock fragments at high-velocity flows will help to design protective structures against natural disasters.


      • 2016-2018 Crack Propagation and Fracture Mechanism in Brittle Materials

    • Shock-induced fracking of rocks – shock waves (and its energy) can be used for fracking the rock formations, an alternative approach to conventional water-based fracking.

    • Crack initiation and propagation occur in material under static and dynamic loading,

    • Crack growth happens in brittle rocks when the applied load (static water pressure) exceeds the threshold energy the incipient cracks or flaws possess.

    • Under shock or impact loading, crack initiation mainly happens due to the impulsive force and crack propagation happens subsequently due to compressed gas flow.
